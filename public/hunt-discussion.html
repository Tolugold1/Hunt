1. What you're actually building

It reads like one product, but it's really two products that share a spine. Being honest about that early stops you from building a tangled monolith.

Product A — Job Hunt Autopilot

Ingest a resume → discover matching jobs across sites → draft a tailored cover letter/email → apply (email send or form fill) on a schedule.

resume parsingjob discoverymatchingauto-apply

Product B — Social Presence Booster

Find/curate articles → generate a platform-native post → schedule & publish to the user's IG / X / Facebook with their permission.

content discoverypost generationOAuth publishingscheduler

The shared spine is: user accounts, a "hunt" definition (what to look for), a scheduler/job queue, an LLM content generator, connected credentials/integrations, and an activity log. Build the spine once; the two products plug into it.

Reframe of the core concept: "Hunt" = a saved search + a rule + an action that runs on a schedule. A job hunt and a content hunt are the same primitive with different sources and different actions. Designing around that primitive keeps the system clean.

2. The reality check (read this first)

This is the part most "auto-apply" projects ignore and then hit a wall on. None of it kills the idea — but it shapes the design heavily.

LinkedIn & Indeed actively fight automation. They have no public "apply" API, their Terms of Service prohibit scraping and bots, and they use sophisticated bot-detection (device fingerprinting, behavioral analysis, CAPTCHAs). Auto-applying through them at scale risks the user's account getting banned. Treat them as assist, not full-auto (see below).
"Email your CV to this address" jobs are your sweet spot. These have no anti-bot wall. Drafting a tailored email + attaching a resume + sending from the user's mailbox is fully automatable, legal, and genuinely valuable. Start here — it's exactly your own workflow.
Social platforms gate posting behind official APIs and app review. Instagram & Facebook posting requires the Meta Graph API + a reviewed app + a Business/Creator account. X (Twitter) posting requires paid API tiers. There is no shortcut that's both reliable and ToS-compliant — budget for the API approval process.

Practical doctrine: split every integration into one of three tiers.

TierWhat it meansExamples
Full-autoLegal + no anti-bot wall. System acts end-to-end.Email-to-apply jobs · RSS/Greenhouse/Lever/Workable job boards (they have APIs/feeds) · Meta & X posting via official APIs
AssistedSystem prepares everything; user does the final click (1-click handoff with pre-filled data).Indeed/LinkedIn form applies · sites with CAPTCHAs · custom ATS forms
Manual / link-outSystem surfaces the job & a tailored draft, then links the user out.Anything with aggressive bot detection or login walls

3. Core modules

1 · Identity & Profile

Accounts, resume upload + structured parse (skills, titles, years, locations), preferences, and the user's sending mailbox connection.

2 · Hunt definitions

Saved searches: keywords, location, remote, salary, sources to use, schedule (cron), and the action (apply / draft-only / notify).

3 · Source connectors

Pluggable adapters: ATS feeds (Greenhouse, Lever, Workable), job-board APIs, RSS, and email-apply listings. One interface, many implementations.

4 · Matching engine

Score a job against the resume (embeddings + rules). Filters noise, ranks, dedupes, avoids re-applying to the same role.

5 · Generator (LLM)

Tailored cover-letter emails, application answers, and social posts — grounded in the resume/article so it isn't generic.

6 · Action executor

Send email (with resume attached), submit form (assisted/headless browser), or publish post. Idempotent & logged.

7 · Scheduler & queue

The heartbeat. Runs hunts at user-set times, rate-limits, retries, and respects daily caps so the user looks human.

8 · Activity & review

Dashboard: what was found, what was sent, what's pending approval, response tracking. Trust is built here.

The key decision: everything heavy runs as background workers off a queue, never inside a web request. Discovery, LLM generation, and sending are all async, retried, and rate-limited. The web app only enqueues and displays.

6. The job-application flow

  1. Setup (once): user uploads resume → parsed into structured profile; connects their sending mailbox via OAuth; sets preferences (titles, locations, remote, salary, sites).
  2. Define a hunt: "Backend roles, remote, Nigeria/EU, run every weekday 9am, max 10 applications/day, draft-and-send for email jobs, draft-only for form jobs."
  3. Discovery (worker): connectors pull new listings from enabled sources; dedupe against history.
  4. Match & rank: score each job vs resume; keep those above a threshold.
  5. Generate: for each kept job, Claude writes a tailored cover-letter email grounded in the resume + JD (not a generic template).
  6. Action by tier:
    • Email job → attach resume, send from user's mailbox (optionally after user approval for the first N).
    • Form job → Playwright pre-fills; user gets a "review & submit" link.
    • Walled → surface job + draft in dashboard; link out.
  7. Log & track: every application recorded; later, parse replies in the mailbox to track responses/interviews.
Approval mode matters for trust. Default new users to "draft, I approve, then send". Only unlock full-auto once they trust the quality. This also keeps you on the right side of "the user authorized each action."

7. The social-posting flow

  1. User connects IG/X/Facebook/LinkedIn via OAuth (each platform's official flow).
  2. User defines a content hunt: topics/keywords, sources (RSS, news APIs, newsletters), cadence, and tone/voice.
  3. Worker discovers articles → Claude generates a platform-native post (caption + hashtags for IG, thread for X, etc.), optionally an image.
  4. Human-in-the-loop queue: posts land in an approval queue. User approves/edits → scheduler publishes at the set time via the platform API.
Compliance reality: auto-posting copyrighted article content can be a problem — generate commentary/summaries with attribution, not copy-paste. And platform app review will ask exactly how you use posting permissions, so keep it clean and user-initiated.

8. Legal, ToS & ethics — design constraints, not afterthoughts

  • Scraping: prefer official APIs/feeds (Greenhouse, Lever, Workable, RSS) over scraping LinkedIn/Indeed. Where you must scrape, scrape only what's permitted and rate-limit hard.
  • Acting as the user: always under explicit user authorization, from the user's own accounts/mailbox. You're an assistant tool, not an impersonation service.
  • Credentials: use OAuth, never store raw passwords. Encrypt tokens at rest. This is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have.
  • Rate & volume caps: daily limits per user protect their accounts and keep quality high. "Spray 500 applications" is a feature that gets users banned and tanks reputation — don't build it.
  • Data privacy: resumes are sensitive PII. Clear retention/deletion, encryption, and a privacy policy from day one.
  • Honesty in applications: the generator must not fabricate experience. Ground everything in the actual resume.

9. MVP & phasing

Resist building both products at once. Here's the order that gets you something real and demo-able fastest, each phase shippable on its own.

1
Email-apply MVP (the wedge). Resume upload + parse · connect Gmail · one source of "email-to-apply" jobs (or manual paste) · Claude drafts the email · user approves · send with resume attached · log it. This alone replicates your personal workflow and is fully legal/automatable. Ship this first.
2
Discovery + scheduling. Add 2–3 API/feed-based job sources, the matching engine, and the scheduler so hunts run on their own. Now it's "autopilot," not a one-off helper.
3
Assisted form-fill. Playwright pre-fills application forms with a review-and-submit handoff. Extends coverage to the big boards without crossing auto-submit lines.
4
Social presence module. Reuse the spine (hunts, generator, scheduler) for content discovery + approval queue + publishing via official APIs. Start with one platform (X or LinkedIn are easiest to get approved).
5
Polish & trust. Response tracking, analytics (application→reply→interview rates, post engagement), multi-user, billing.

10. Monetization & positioning

Pricing model

Freemium: free = X applications/posts per month + draft-only. Paid tiers = higher volume, full-auto, more sources, response analytics. LLM + browser automation are your real costs, so meter on actions.

Who it's for

Active job-seekers (esp. high-volume markets), freelancers/creators who need consistent posting, and bootcamp grads. Your own use case is the proof.

Portfolio angle

Shows off: queue/worker architecture, LLM integration, OAuth with multiple providers, browser automation, and a real product story. Strong signal for backend/full-stack roles.

Moat / differentiation

Quality of tailoring + trust (human-in-loop) beats spray-and-pray competitors. "Increase application rate without nuking your reputation."

11. Open questions & risks to decide on

  • Single language or split? All-TypeScript (fastest solo) vs Python backend (better scraping/ML libs). I lean all-TS for v1.
  • How much full-auto to allow? The more auto, the higher the value and the risk. Recommend approval-first by default.
  • Which sources realistically? Audit which of your sites (Indeed, LinkedIn, MyJobMag, CryptoJobsList) have feeds/APIs vs need assisted/manual. CryptoJobsList & ATS-backed boards are likely easier than LinkedIn/Indeed.
  • Social app review timeline. Meta/X app approval can take weeks — start that process early if Product B matters to you.
  • Deliverability. Sending from the user's own mailbox (OAuth) sidesteps most spam issues vs sending from your servers.
  • Cost control. LLM + headless browsers + storage add up. Cache aggressively, use small models for filtering, big models only for final writing.

12. Recommended next step

Build Phase 1 (Email-apply MVP) end-to-end first. It's the smallest slice that fully replicates your own job-application workflow, has zero ToS landmines, and proves the spine (auth → resume parse → LLM draft → mailbox send → log). Everything else bolts onto that.
